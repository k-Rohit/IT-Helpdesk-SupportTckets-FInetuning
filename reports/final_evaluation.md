## Summary

Each stage of the pipeline shows a measurable, explainable shift in behavior. The **base model** frequently echoes the question, hallucinates unrelated scenarios, or leaks training-data artifacts ("Answer according to:"), and rarely stops cleanly. The **SFT model** reliably adopts a professional support-ticket tone and stops cleanly, but leans heavily on asking clarifying questions rather than giving direct resolutions -- a direct reflection of the source dataset's own patterns. The **DPO model** pushes the tone toward more direct, actionable troubleshooting steps on well-represented ticket categories (network, printer, billing, SaaS performance), improving on SFT in 6 of 10 cases. However, DPO also introduced clear regressions on two categories that were already underrepresented earlier in the pipeline -- security/compliance tickets and niche hardware-pairing questions -- producing an incoherent run-on response and an outright hallucination (a fabricated 'Blu-ray player' scenario) respectively. This is attributed to limited category coverage in the ~80-example preference dataset after quality filtering, rather than a flaw in the DPO method itself. Overall, DPO improved directness and helpfulness on the majority of common ticket types, at the cost of some category-specific regressions that a larger, more evenly-distributed preference dataset would likely resolve.
